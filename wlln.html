Cover Letter

Language has always been a vital part of who I am and my story. However, I didn’t truly realize the impact that language has had in my life until I sat down to write this essay. This essay challenged me to think deeper about my own language and literacy journey. I am grateful to have had the opportunity to reflect on this journey.

When I wrote this essay, I intended my audience to be other immigrants, specifically children of immigrants who must had experience what I have experienced. I wanted my audience to know that they were not alone in their struggles, that it’s okay if they felt those same feelings of fear and sadness I felt during the first few years in America. So, in my essay, I delved deeper into my feelings, from what I was feeling when I entered my first classroom in America to what I was feeling when I chose my book for silent reading time. By describing in depth my feelings in these moments, I could take my audience on a trip down memory lane, when they felt (or maybe still do feel) this type of way. In addition, I knew a lot of my classmates were going to be the ones reading my essay and many of them could relate to my story, so I felt it was important to tell it. On the other hand, I would hope that regardless of a personal connection to my narrative, the audience takes something away from story, about the struggles that everyday immigrants endure.

In writing this essay, some of the most meaningful insights I have gained are that the more description and dialogue, the better. I found myself thoroughly enjoying providing these descriptions and revisiting these memories. I have come to realize how much both my writing and reading skills have improved. In writing this narrative, I have started to become more vulnerable in my writing. This essay helped me greatly improve the description in my writing. Rhetorical situation, purpose, and audience have most impacted my learning and writing practices. For example, in writing this essay, I have become very aware of these tools. As I was writing it, I pictured the triangular diagram we completed in class. I knew that I was utilizing a lot of pathos in my own writing by providing extensive details of my feelings and actions. In addition, I wanted to make sure that my essay was easy to read, like Amy Tan mentions in “Mother Tongue.”

This assignment has helped me achieve the following Course Learning Outcomes: “develop strategies for reading, drafting, collaborating, revising, and editing” and “engage in the collaborative and social aspects of writing processes.” During the process of completing this assignment, I, along with my classmates, spent a great deal of time revising and editing our essays. The peer reviews helped me develop strategies for reading, drafting, collaborating, revising, and editing. Additionally, during the in-class peer review sessions, I began to engage in the collaborative and social aspects of the writing process. In discussing my essay with my peers, I developed a friendship and trust with them. Many of us found that we connected with each other’s pieces of writing. Moreover, the spoken language and literacy narrative assignment helped me get to know my classmates better. Overall, writing my language and literacy narrative was a great learning experience.


Second Draft

Primary School, Standard 1
I wish I had known how much I would regret it. It was 11AM and I was standing on line for the teacher to check homework. Sweat started slowly rolling down the right side of my face. At this point, both of my hands were drowned in it. My heart began to beat faster. I tried to keep my hands from shaking.


I got one step closer. There were now 4 more people in front of me.

I will be fine. He will forget by lunch, I reassured myself, half believing myself. 3, 2, 1...

“Next!” Mr. Akter barked.

“Where’s your homework?” He growled, seeing my empty hands.

“Uhh...mhm...you see....I...” My throat had closed. Somehow my voice had taken a vacation.

“You what?!” He eyed me suspiciously.

“I didn’t do the homework.”

“Hold out your palm,” He muttered.

“I am so sorry-”

“HOLD OUT YOUR HAND!” This time, he bellowed.

I slowly put out my palm, hoping the bell would ring. Maybe the principal would come in.

Oh please. He would just watch and have himself a show.

WHACK! I felt the cold ruler slam against my palm. My palm turned a deep shade of pink. I walked back to my desk, looking down at my feet as the other kids laughed.

When I was younger, I used to hate school. I remember how much I dreaded going. I never felt excited to learn or make new friends, but instead, I felt scared. The teachers intimidated me. They towered over me, holding long, sharp rulers in their hands, ready for their next victim. The first time I forgot my homework was the first time I received the painful WHACK! on my palm. In Bangladesh, there was no rule against hitting students and as a result, my motivation to learn was fear more than anything else.


2nd Grade
When I was eight years old, my family and I moved to America. All my life, I had only known one country, language, and culture. But suddenly, I was thrown into this whole new place. The first day of second grade was the first time I attended school in America, so it was a big day indeed. It was a chilly but sunny fall morning. I held on tightly to my dad’s hand and made my way across the school field to the towering, red brick building. As we made our way to the classroom, I saw the other kids running up and hugging their friends and shouting what sounded to me like a jumble of gibberish. It all sounded so foreign.


As my dad and I reached closer and closer to the classroom, 201, my heart began to race faster, and my throat dried up. I tried to take deep breaths, but it was as if I had forgotten how to. It was as if someone had placed a brick on my chest. We finally reached the classroom, which smelled of curry, a familiar smell that made me feel a bit better. The desks were formed in a rectangular shape around half the classroom and on each desk, I could see names scribbled at the top. My dad walked me to my desk and bid me goodbye and because he knew how nervous I was, said to me, “cinta koreo na.” I watched my dad leave and the other kids file in with their parents. On my desk, there was a cup of crayons and a coloring book. On the cover, I saw a picture of an elephant. Just as I began to open the book and take out the crayons out of the cup, I heard an elder woman’s voice. I can recall and mostly assume that she uttered something along the lines of:


“Welcome, class! My name is Ms. Perez and I will be your teacher for second grade. In a second, I will ask each of you to go around and introduce yourself.”

Immediately, I panicked. Since arriving in America, my cousins had taught me a few sayings and phrases, but the only one that echoed in my head was “I will kick you up in the sky!” It was one my cousins laughed at, but one I had grown quite fond of. I searched my brain for words and landed on “My name is Samira and I am from Bangladesh.” This was the first time I was speaking these words in my “broken” and desi accent in front of a group of strangers. So, I kept rehearsing in my head. My name is Samira and I am from Bangladesh. My name is Samira and I am from Bangladesh. My name is Samira and I am from Bangladesh... I wanted my first impression in front of these sea of unknown faces to be a good one. I didn’t want them to think that the only Bengali kid in class was “weird” or “dumb.”

There were several people that introduced themselves, and then it was my turn. Oh, boy. Here I go. I slowly stood up and stuttered, “Hi, my...name...is Samira and I-I am from Bangladesh.” After I finished, I saw the teacher smile at me. There weren’t any sharp rulers in her hand. There was only kindness in her eyes. The brick on my chest felt a bit lighter.

4th Grade
Fast forward to two years later. In fourth grade, my speaking was much better than it had been two years prior. But I was still struggling with pretty everything else. I still felt that I was behind all my classmates. I was barely passing all my classes. The looks of disappointment every time my parents saw my report cards were soul-crushing. There was one person that was even more disappointed than they were. It was me. I kept having flashbacks of school in Bangladesh, of the end of the year ceremony, of the moments when I would be the twentieth person to walk onto the stage to receive my report card. While my friends came in first or second place and excitedly went up first to the stage to receive their prize, I looked down at my feet and hoped to be anywhere but there.


I was much happier than I had been in school in Bangladesh, but it wasn’t exactly easy feeling like the “dumb” kid in class. Everyone seemed so much smarter.

At the beginning of the year, we were assigned a reading level, represented by different colors. I remember barely getting through the reading assessment, and being assigned to read at the red level, which was far below the grade level.

You’re dumb, and everyone here can see it, I said to myself one day during reading time, when everyone was choosing books from their designated reading level. I picked up a first-grade level picture book while the other kids conversed over The Babysitters Club.

“Oh my god?! Did you read the fourth one?! It’s so good,” one girl hissed to her friend.

“I really recommend you read The Babysitters Club. You are more than capable of reading chapter books as these,” I overheard my teacher tell another girl.

I opened Frog and Toad are Friends. “Frog walked into the house. It was dark…”


Present Day
Flashforward to present day: I am a freshman in college, ready to take my language and literacy skills to the next level. Until I started writing more about my language and literacy journey, I didn't realize the great impact that language has had in my life.


Looking back, I wish I could give my younger self a hug. The first few years in America were truly the hardest. Everyone, from my teachers to my parents were pressuring me to do just as well as the kids who had grown up speaking, writing, and reading the same language their entire lives. It’s unfair to expect this level of proficiency in English so quickly from anyone, let alone a child.

I am sure I am not alone when I say that moving across the world and adjusting to a new environment and culture is tough. Every year, hundreds of thousands of people immigrate to the United States. They leave behind everyone and everything they know in search of a better life. Some, like my parents, move because they want a better life for their children. Others move because they have no other choice.

I wish society was kinder to immigrants. Over the past couple of years, the level of hatred against immigrants has risen significantly, not just in the U.S., but in the world. Humanity has lacked empathy towards immigrants. It makes me sad that we always must prove our loyalty to our country, that the same country we love and adore doesn’t always love us back. Sometimes, people fail to see that our country is better because immigrants are in it. Without immigrants, the U.S. wouldn’t be the “melting pot of cultures” it always markets itself as. America is not just one culture, religion, or language. It’s all the three hundred fifty languages, and it’s all the hundreds of cultures and religions and races. That’s what makes America great, not hate or fear.

WHACK!

But to me, he seemed like a towering giant, ready to destroy all that angers him in his path. Mr. Akter was wearing a brown vest that looked like it had been worn for a very, very long time. He loved that damn vest. He also wore a mean, cruel scowl on his face.

Present Day
Flashforward to present day: I am a freshman in college, ready to take my language and literacy skills to the next level. Until I started writing more about my language and literacy journey, I didn't realize the great impact that language has had in my life. Looking back, I wish I could give my younger self a hug. That day I received the WHACK! truly traumatized me. Even to this day, if I see a teacher being disrespected, something inside me dies. Although hitting students still may be allowed in many parts of Bangladesh and throughout the world, it is NOT okay. A child shouldn’t be hit in order to be disciplined. In many South Asian cultures, it is normalized for children to be not only hit, but beaten when they are younger. This truly traumatizes children, even way into their adulthoods, even as they are raising their own children. It’s a vicious cycle that will never end until our communities collectively began to recognize the impact of a beating or hit or slap. I know that for me, at least, the painful WHACK! on that faithful definitely has shaped me in ways I could have never imagined.
