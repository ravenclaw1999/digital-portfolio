Reflection on ENGL 110: Phases of Writing

During the first three phases of English 110, I obtained many valuable insights into myself as a writer. In writing the three core assignments for each phase, I learned to become more aware of my own skills and flaws as a writer. I might not have perfected every skill or course learning objective, but I know that I definitely improved, and most importantly, I started to find my voice as a writer.

In phase one, one of the most important course learning outcomes I achieved was the following: recognize the role of language attitudes and standards in empowering, oppressing, and hierarchizing languages and their users, and be open to communicating across different languages and cultures. Prior to this phase, I knew that language had played some role in my life. Afterall, it is one of the most important tools of communication for humans. However, I truly began to understand and appreciate my own experience with language during this phase. In writing my own Writing Language and Literacy Narrative, I learned to embrace my own “Englishes.” I also started to utilize lots of description and imagery in my writing as well, which was a great step in my improvement as a writer. Amy Tan’s “Mother Tongue” especially spoke to me. It struck to me how much I related to her article. As I was reading her article, I found myself laughing aloud and connecting deeply with what Tan grapples with in her writing. I connected with her beliefs that her mother’s English wasn’t broken, rather that it was perfectly natural. As a child of immigrant parents, I related to feeling upset that my parents’ English was somehow considered “broken” and need of fixing by the rest of society. Furthermore, I also found Saleem’s “Why I keep speaking up, even when people mock my accent” TED Talk to be quite relatable and connected with his message about language.

Another course learning objective I started to achieve in phase one was the following: engage in the collaborative and social aspects of writing processes. The Spoken Language and Literacy Narrative, though a bit frightening sharing a personal story with the class, was a great way that my classmates and I got to know each other a bit better, especially during these virtual times. In addition to connecting with my own roots, culture, and language, during phase one, I became much better at recognizing the rhetorical situation and started to even become better at analyzing the rhetorical tools used by a writer to effectively convey their argument. I became better at this through completing the rhetorical situation worksheets for “Mother Tongue” and “Why I keep speaking up, even when people mock my accent.” Even though it may not have been the most fun, doing the rhetorical worksheets were helpful for beginning to recognize how an author constructs their argument using evidence and the rhetorical appeals of pathos, logos, and ethos. Additionally, I vividly remember constructing the rhetorical situation triangle in class. Though the focus on rhetorical analysis was greater in phase two, I began to achieve this second course learning outcome: explore and analyze, in writing and reading, a variety of genres and rhetorical situations.

In phase two, there was a much greater emphasis on rhetorical analysis and what an author does, rather than just say, in their writing. It was definitely much more difficult to start to move away from simply summarizing what an author is saying in their writing to what the author is doing in their writing using the many rhetorical tools available to them. In analyzing June Jordan’s “Nobody Mean More to Me Than You and the Life of Willie Jordan,” another interesting piece on the role of language in our society, I learned to identify what Jordan was doing in her writing. For instance, in the text, she was demonstrating that by neglecting Black English and other forms of English, society was also neglecting the communities that use different forms of English. Jordan demonstrates this by utilizing the rhetorical appeals of pathos, logos, and ethos. She provides the personal story of Willie Jordan’s brother’s experience with police brutality and how that personally impacted her and her students. In analyzing Jordan’s essay, I was beginning to achieve the following course learning objective: recognize and practice key rhetorical terms and strategies when engaged in writing situations. This one is another crucial learning objective I came upon during the three phases, as I began to identify the significant rhetorical terms and strategies in what I was reading, and in my own writing. The Rhetorical Analysis Essay required me to identify what the author was doing in their writing. Since I choose to write about “Mother Tongue,” I had to delve into how Tan demonstrated the importance of language in her relationship with her mother through the use of personal stories and anecdotes, thereby utilizing pathos to effectively convey her argument to the audience.

During phase two, I also achieved the following learning adjective: develop strategies for reading, drafting, collaborating, revising, and editing. Peer review was a tool we as a class had used in phase one, but it was especially important during this phase, as we were having to critique each other’s analysis of an author’s writing. We had to especially pay attention to whether we were merely summarizing in our writing, or actually analyzing and illustrating what the author was doing in their writing. I had found the following format to be especially beneficial: the author [VERB] [IDEA] by [EXPLAIN HOW]. Learning to become better at collaborating and giving feedback was really useful in successfully analyzing the text I choose. In addition, I found the collaborative process to be a nice pace of change and a great way to connect with my classmates during these virtual times.

Phase three was perhaps the difficult, yet most important phase of all. It was the phase of the research essay. It was the phase in which I encountered the bulk of these course learning objectives: understand and use print and digital technologies to address a range of audiences; locate research sources (including academic journal articles, magazine and newspaper articles) in the library’s databases or archives and on the Internet and evaluate them for credibility, accuracy, timeliness, and bias; compose texts that integrate your stance with appropriate sources using strategies such as summary, critical analysis, interpretation, synthesis, and argumentation; practice systematic application of citation conventions. The first part of this phase involved learning how to actually search for sources effectively and learning how to use the sources available to me such as the library databases, including Jstor. Learning to insert certain phases into Google (e.g. putting something in quotes versus without quotes or using words such as “or” to sort through search results). The second part of this phase was utilizing and synthesizing the sources I found. Synthesizing sources rather than simply summarizing was a hard task that I picked up on as I began to analyze and connect my sources together. Last, but not least, I had to then use all my synthesis and sources to actually put together my research essay. I struggled quite a bit with forming a clear and solid thesis, as I had many claims I wanted to explore in the essay. In the end, I structured my essay so that each part of my thesis flowed into the next and connected together.

I admit that I still have to learn a lot about writing, and about myself as a writer. But without a doubt, I gained very meaningful and insightful skills during the first three phases of English 110. Each phase became more difficult and challenged me as a writer and student. With each phase, I learned so much more about how to implement certain strategies in my own writing to effectively convey my argument. Overall, it has been a great learning experience.
